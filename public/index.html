Introduction
    Beloved is a renowned novel from Toni Morrison, one of the most prolific and influential writers of the 20th century. Set in Cincinnati, Ohio in 1873, the story blends unique portions of magical realism with incredibly empathetic and multi-dimensional characters while also touching on heart-wrenching topics like child loss, slavery, and, most of all, love.

    While delving into the novel, I was intrigued by the recurring idea of memory and trauma, particularly the idea of intergenerational trauma carried by the main character. I have always been interested in aspects of psychology and Beloved provides a mature insight into the world of the characters’ psyches. Sethe as a character especially drew me in due to her struggle with the past and her attempts to deal with her trauma. Portions of the book written in Sethe’s stream of consciousness aided in developing her character and establishing the recurring idea of her “rememory” as well as the generational trauma caused by her history as a slave and her attempt to escape the fugitive slave catchers. Memory and the ideas around dealing with trauma are an integral part of Beloved and what I chose to focus on in this project.

    For the informational genre, I decided to focus on the Fugitive Slave Acts of 1793 and 1850. Providing context for such an abhorred era of U.S. history is crucial in understanding the foundation of intergenerational trauma for many ex-slaves. Sethe dealt with slave catchers in her history, leading to the ultimate act of murdering her child and her primary source of trauma. The Fugitive Slave Act created intense fear among all African Americans for a plethora of reasons and the psychological damage reverberates in all characters throughout Beloved, not just Sethe. The importance of Ohio as a setting and its surface level title as a “free state” also plays a role in Sethe’s history as a runaway slave. In providing context on these two topics, I hope to explore the historical reasons behind a culture of fear and shared trauma among ex-slaves and the families of those people.

    The final chapter of Beloved is a unique passage that provides a huge shift in perspective and ideas around trauma, love, and moving forward. I decided to provide an in-depth analysis on the passage, exploring its complex messages about how to best deal with abuse and trauma as the book comes to a close. Memory, trauma, and growth are all very present throughout the final chapter as the community strives to move on from Beloved. The chapter is unique in its delivery of the novel’s overall messages of healing and growth, however, as the community acts as a showcase of how not to heal wounds left by past trauma. Cryptic but full of Morrison’s talented literary skill, the last chapter is a perfect encapsulation of the book.

    For the creative piece, I wanted to step in the shoes of Denver as a baby during the scene in which Sethe kills Beloved. Denver is such a crucial character in Beloved especially concerning the idea of intergenerational trauma as Denver is the person who inherits the trauma from Sethe. I wanted to explore what it would be like for a baby to experience such an act with both a sense of confusion, childlike innocence, but also a lingering sense of horror. Sethe’s decision to kill Beloved was a large part of why Denver was so isolated as an adult and I wanted to explore her thoughts during that scene. I took inspiration from Chapter 22 of Beloved in which the novel takes the perspective of baby Beloved as she is in the land of the dead. The writing style in that chapter stuck with me due to its oddities and I decided to explore that world a little further.

    Each of the genres touch on the ideas of trauma, whether it’s understanding the historical reasons behind intergenerational trauma or a first-hand perspective of a traumatic experience. I believe that everyone should have the proper skills to deal with such memories and events and I hope that this website helps portray Toni Morrison’s message in a new and interactive manner.
Information:
Fugitive Slave Act
The Fugitive Slave Acts legalized the inhumane treatment of both enslaved and free people, creating an intense abolitionist opposition and a culture of fear among African Americans.
CONTEXT
Initially passed in 1793, the Fugitive Slave Act stated that slave owners retained the right to search for escaped slaves in the borders of free states while also making the act of protecting escaped slaves illegal.
In 1850, Congress passed the Fugitive Slave Act which further enforced the capture of escaped slaves, forcing citizens to aid in the recapturing of runaways.
The new Fugitive Slave Act also mobilized the federal government to capture escaped slaves, giving them the right to issue warrants integral in capturing runaways.
As a result of the act, the Underground Railroads gained immense popularity as a method to transfer escaped slaves.
Furthermore, resistance grew among abolitionists as they became enraged with the government's advocacy of pro-slave legislation.
African Americans grew afraid to be mistakenly recaptured themselves while also being fearful towards helping other escaped slaves due to the possible repercussions that could accompany such aid.
OHIO: A "FREE" STATE
Ohio was considered a free state in the 1800s, but not all slaves were considered free within the state's borders.
While Ohio did prohibit the selling and purchasing of slaves, it did not prohibit the act of indentured servitude.
Slave owners were allowed to bring their slaves into Ohio for an undetermined amount of time before they were freed. The threat of being sent back to enslaved states kept slaves under the influence of their master, further escalating the feeling of fear and control.
Cincinatti as a city provided rewards for the recapturing of slaves, fueling the idea that despite Ohio being a free state, it was complacent to the pro-slavery actions of the nation.
Slaves believed in two versions of "freedom", one version granted by the slave owner and the federal government and one version that remained unattainable--true freedom. Beloved brings up this distinction between freedom as an allusion to the facade of freedom that many slaves faced as a result of the Fugitive Slave Acts.
Analysis:
Beloved's Final Chapter

Passage
Analysis

    In Toni Morrison’s novel Beloved, the final chapter serves to conclude the ideas of love, trauma and acceptance. Beloved has been banished from 124 by the collective force of the community of women, saving Sethe from her parasitic grasp. Beloved, in this final chapter, symbolizes the ubiquitous mental affliction on African Americans caused by countless years of slavery and dehumanization. In the final pages, Morrison takes on a disembodied perspective and reveals the fate of Beloved along with the effect that she had on the community around 124, ultimately concluding that, in order to truly heal traumatic wounds and prevent intergenerational trauma, people must not dwell on the painful woes of the past but instead move forward through an emotional acknowledgement of one's history, while also recognizing the benefits of forgetting a traumatizing past.

    At the beginning of the passage, the narrator establishes Beloved as a widely impactful and acknowledged figure, yet paradoxically forgettable despite her impact on the community. Morrison describes the contradictory circumstances of Beloved’s notoriety, stating, “Everybody knew what she was called, but nobody anywhere knew her name.” The community of 124 knew of Beloved and the damage she caused Sethe, but they didn’t truly know who she was and opted to forget her. Her lack of a name symbolizes not only the struggle that mothers experience during slavery, opting to not name their children due to the likelihood of them dying young, but it also signifies Morrison’s focus on hopefulness for a brighter future. Rather than dwelling on Beloved’s name and who she is, the community moves on. Beloved’s legacy is forgotten and her memory fades into nature, “[erupting] into her separate parts.” The idea that she splits into separate parts demonstrates Beloved’s multifaceted meaning, representing not only the lives lost in the horrific era of slavery, but also as a universal symbol for grief and pain as a result of historical trauma. Just as Beloved separates into distinct parts, some representing grief and pain while others represent love, those dealing with trauma must also separate their history into different pieces in order to more effectively deal with their abuse. More broadly, the community’s focus on forgetting Beloved supports the idea that forgetting a painful past can be an effective way of dealing with trauma, moving away from history into a brighter future filled with hope. Furthermore, the narrator states that the community believed that “remembering seemed unwise,” willingly forgetting Beloved because thinking about her is not productive to repairing the damage she left. This assertion demonstrates that ruminating on abuse suffered in the past is an unhelpful and damaging process which prevents one from moving forward.

    Despite the community’s aim to forget Beloved, the narrator depicts Beloved as ubiquitous and hidden within every inch of the community’s lives, illustrating the difficulty in forgetting past pain or trauma. The narrator describes that, “Occasionally, however, the rustle of a skirt hushes when they wake,” insinuating that the spirit of Beloved follows the lives of all those who came into contact with her long after she had been banished. Even after forgetting Beloved, her impact on the community remains hidden in the hushed corners of their lives, appearing in small moments when the community seems to finally forget her. Her constant appearance in the lives of others reflects how traumatic and painful memories are never truly forgotten but rather stowed in the subconscious of those who experienced them. Memories of abuse may seem to fade, but they always seem to appear in associations made many years ago. Photos of family, the movement of fabric, a breeze during sleep can all bring back long-forgotten memories of that abuse. Beloved had been banished for a long time, but her impact still shakes the people she interacted with. Similarly, even after slavery had been outlawed for decades, the remnants of the physical and emotional oppression still haunted the lives of many African Americans. Trauma stays with people even long after the event occurred, requiring those to find a way to deal with the memories in a healthy manner.

    Throughout the passage, the narrator repeats the phrase “this is not a story to pass on” in order to establish a contradicting idea between telling Sethe and Beloved’s story while also saying to keep it hidden, ultimately illustrating that passing on a traumatic story can be a way to heal trauma when presented in a respectful and meaningful manner. The community of African Americans who experienced Beloved did not carry on her tale and tried to heal their grief and pain through forgetting, keeping her story inside. Morrison, through presenting Beloved’s story as a symbol of the lives lost during the tragic struggle of slavery, thus contradicts the narrator’s assertion that her story is not to be passed on. While the community strives to forget her, Morrison pushes the reader to remember Sethe and Beloved’s story as a way of honoring the African American lives that were lost as a result of enslavement. By writing a story about a historical figure which fought against the sins of slavery, Sethe and Beloved’s story is passed on, demonstrating that remembering painful times can be an effective way of dealing with suffering when done in a respectful manner, focused primarily on how to heal those wounds.

    Beloved’s last chapter assumes a disembodied perspective, focusing on the disappearance and lasting effects of Beloved on the community around 124. Beloved, acting as a symbol not only for pain and grief but also love and hopefulness, helps conclude the ideas of how to heal wounds left by trauma and the importance of dealing with past abuse in a meaningful and healthy way.
